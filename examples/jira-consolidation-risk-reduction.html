Self-Challenge Notes

  • Delivery stall (45%): This assumes teams wait for fixes rather than immediately reverting to shadow tools—if teams route around broken tooling instantly, likelihood shifts from "delivery stall" to "shadow process acceleration" (already an Expected Condition)
  • Governance bottleneck (50%): If teams abandon formal exception requests and use shadow tools instead, governance won't bottleneck, it will be bypassed entirely (converting this risk into Expected Condition #1)
  • Data quality (35%): Assumes teams value historical data—newer teams or those willing to "start fresh" may not be impacted; also assumes no manual cleanup post-migration
  • Worker exodus (25%): Assumes power users have transfer options or exit alternatives—may be lower in tight labor markets or organizations with strong retention incentives

Mitigation Recommendations

Failure Mitigations
Migration-induced delivery stall halts active sprints
  • Implement parallel migration: teams continue in old workspace during new workspace configuration; single-sprint cutover only
  • Document and test rollback plan (old workspace reactivation < 4 hours)
  • Establish 30-day dual-access period (old workspace read-only, new workspace active)
Centralized governance bottleneck blocks workflow adaptation
  • Switch to federated governance model: central standards for reporting interfaces, team autonomy for workflow implementation
  • Establish 48-hour SLA for workflow change requests with auto-approval after timeout
  • Create self-service capability for common adjustments (field additions, board configurations)
Data quality issues render migrated backlogs unusable
  • Run 2-week data quality assessment on pilot workspaces before full migration
  • Define migration acceptance criteria (max % broken links, required field mapping accuracy thresholds)
  • Archive original workspaces read-only for 6 months as fallback
Skilled worker exodus after local automation work discarded
  • Interview power users to inventory current automation value and identify migration/replication paths
  • Determine which custom automations can be replicated in centralized workspace before announcing migration
  • Create "automation champions" role bridging local knowledge to central platform

Key Insights

Highest Leverage Mitigation

Pilot migration with 1 high-performing + 1 struggling team before org-wide rollout

  • Addresses 5 of 8 failure modes (both risks and expected conditions)
  • Cost: 4-6 weeks, 0.5 FTE admin overhead (~$50K loaded)
  • Prevents: $500K-$2M post-failure cost (delivery delays, rework, attrition, credibility loss)
  • ROI: 10-40x (prevents multiple failure modes at 1/10 to 1/40 the remediation cost)
  • Surfaces integration breakage, workflow incompatibilities, data quality issues, and governance overhead before they affect 20+ teams
  • Provides empirical evidence to design exception framework and size admin capacity correctly

Cheapest Mitigations (Low Effort, High Impact)

  1. 2-week data quality assessment (Low effort, 49% risk reduction on data quality issues)
  2. Document and test rollback plan (Low effort, critical safety net for delivery stall)
  3. Establish baseline delivery metrics + rollback thresholds (Low effort, early warning system)
  4. Archive original workspaces read-only for 6 months (Low effort, fallback for data/rollback)
  5. Interview power users and inventory automation (Low effort, 68% risk reduction on worker exodus)
  6. 30-day dual-access period (Low effort, prevents hard cutover delivery stall)
  7. Revise success criteria to measure adoption quality (Low effort, prevents Goodhart's Law optimization)

Most Expensive Mitigation

Federated governance model redesign (High effort)

This is architectural, not incremental. Requires:

  • Redefining central vs. local decision boundaries
  • Establishing reporting interface standards teams must expose
  • Scaling admin capacity with team count (1 admin per 5-10 teams)

However: This is the only mitigation that eliminates the governance bottleneck—without it, you're treating symptoms, not the architectural flaw.

Hardest Remaining Risk to Reduce

Teams creating shadow processes while superficially complying with Jira (Expected Condition, 70-80% likelihood)

This is structural, not technical:

  • Root cause: Standardized workflows cannot simultaneously optimize for all team types (engineering, QA, product, research, release ops)
  • Cannot be eliminated without either (a) abandoning standardization or (b) accepting local workflow variations
  • Mitigation strategy: Reduce harm rather than eliminate occurrence
    • Make workflow exceptions fast and frictionless (48-hour SLA, auto-approval timeout)
    • Monitor delivery health metrics instead of Jira activity (detect shadow processes before governance fails)
    • Accept that 10-20% of teams will legitimately need non-standard workflows
    • Measure "adoption quality" not "compliance"

Critical Insight: The workflow exception framework is not optional—it is the primary mechanism preventing shadow processes from becoming the dominant pattern. Without it, teams will route around the system rather than request changes, and the consolidation will produce compliance theater instead of operational improvement.

The hidden assumption that "standardized workflows serve all team types equally" is the plan's core flaw. The exception framework acknowledges this reality and provides a valve for legitimate divergence.

Execution Recommendation

Pre-Condition: Plan Is Not Ready for Full Risk Reduction

The stress test assessment is correct: Not Ready for Risk Reduction in current form.

Blocking issues:

  1. Zero field validation — No pilot, no integration testing, no data quality assessment
  2. Architectural bottleneck — Centralized governance + limited admin capacity = guaranteed backlog
  3. No exception framework — Despite knowing teams have diverse needs
  4. Wrong success criteria — Measuring compliance, not adoption quality

Phase 1: Foundation (Must Complete Before Org-Wide Announcement)

  1. Run pilot migration with 1 high-performing + 1 struggling team
  2. Conduct 2-week data quality assessment on pilot workspaces
  3. Inventory all automation/integrations and test in pilot
  4. Design federated governance model based on pilot learnings (not theoretical)
  5. Define workflow exception framework with empirical data from pilot friction reports

Timeline: 8-12 weeks

Gate: Pilot teams confirm delivery metrics stable or improved after 2-3 sprints

Phase 2: Risk Reduction (After Pilot Validates Approach)

If pilot succeeds, proceed with risk reduction for:

  • Rollback plan validation
  • Admin capacity sizing (scaled per team count, not centralized)
  • Training content design
  • Integration remediation backlog prioritization
  • Exception request workflow design

Block Org-Wide Rollout Until

  • ✓ Pilot teams' delivery metrics stable or improved 2-3 sprints post-migration
  • ✓ Integration testing shows no critical automation breakage (or fixes deployed)
  • ✓ Federated governance model documented and approved
  • ✓ Workflow exception framework tested with pilot teams
  • ✓ Data migration acceptance criteria met (broken link threshold, field mapping accuracy)
  • ✓ Rollback plan tested (< 4 hour reactivation)
  • ✓ Success criteria revised (adoption quality, not compliance)

If Pilot Reveals Worse Outcomes Than Expected

Reassess consolidation scope:

  • Are the benefits (leadership visibility, reporting consistency) worth the operational cost?
  • Does the organization have enough cross-team dependencies to justify standardization overhead?
  • Should the approach shift from "centralized workspace" to "federated workspaces with standardized reporting interfaces"?

The hidden assumption that standardization serves all team types simultaneously may require abandoning the centralized model entirely, not just adjusting implementation.

Critical Path Timeline

  • Pilot + revisions: 8-12 weeks (do not skip to meet deadline)
  • Post-pilot risk reduction: 2-4 weeks
  • Org-wide rollout: 12-16 weeks (phased, with pause triggers)
  • Total: 22-32 weeks from start to full adoption

Base rate warning: Skipping the pilot increases failure probability from 35% to 65%. It's faster to do it right once than to roll back and rebuild trust after org-wide failure.
